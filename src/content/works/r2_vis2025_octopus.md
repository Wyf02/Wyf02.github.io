## Abstract

Cartography has long been recognized as a form of rhetoric—maps don't just represent space, they construct narratives. This paper examines how visual design choices in cartographic representations influence persuasion and perception, using Octopus Maps as a primary case study.

## Introduction

The "Octopus Map" became famous during the COVID-19 pandemic as a representation that visually prioritized certain narratives about transportation networks. This work investigates the deliberate design choices embedded in such visualizations.

## Visual Rhetoric Framework

### Design Choices Examined
- Color schemes and their psychological effects
- Scale distortion and emphasis
- Symbol design and hierarchy
- Information filtering and omission

## Case Study: Octopus Maps

We analyze:
1. Historical evolution of octopus-style cartography
2. Design decisions and their communicative intent
3. Viewer interpretation and misinterpretation
4. Ethical implications of persuasive mapping

## Findings

Our analysis reveals:
- Visual choices are rarely neutral; they embed implicit arguments
- Users often internalize distortions without recognizing them
- Awareness of design choices improves critical viewing

## Discussion

This research contributes to:
- Critical cartography scholarship
- Data visualization ethics
- Visual communication studies
- Responsible design practices

## Implications

Designers must consider the rhetorical impact of their visual choices and communicate assumptions explicitly to users.

## Future Work
